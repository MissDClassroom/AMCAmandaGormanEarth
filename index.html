Step 01 — Discovery

Encountering the Poem

Before analysing, receive the poem fully: watch, listen, and read. Let the voice do its work first.

🛰️

Historical Anchor

On Christmas Eve 1968, astronaut Bill Anders photographed Earth rising above the lunar surface — a first. That image redefined humanity's relationship with its own planet.

🎤

The Poet

Amanda Gorman, born 1998 in Los Angeles. Raised by a single mother, she overcame a speech impediment to become the first U.S. Youth Poet Laureate (2017). She performed "Earthrise" at NASA's launch of Committed to the Earth.

🌍

The Context

2018: the year the IPCC issued its landmark report giving humanity 12 years to limit catastrophic warming. Youth climate activism was surging, with Greta Thunberg about to take the world stage.

🗣️

Spoken Word & Slam

This poem belongs to the spoken word tradition, where rhythm, breath, and body are part of the text. The page version is a score for a live performance — layout directs timing.

The full text

▶ Listen on YouTube
On Christmas Eve, 1968, astronaut Bill Anders
Snapped a photo of the earth
As Apollo 8 orbited the moon.

Those three guys
Were surprised
To see from their eyes
Our planet looked like an earthrise
A blue orb hovering over the moon's gray horizon,
with deep oceans and silver skies.

It was our world's first glance at itself
Our first chance to see a shared reality,
A declared stance and a commonality;

A glimpse into our planet's mirror,
And as threats drew nearer,
Our own urgency became clearer,
As we realize that we hold nothing dearer
than this floating body we all call home.

We've known
That we're caught in the throes
Of climactic changes some say
Will just go away,
While some simply pray
To survive another day;
For it is the obscure, the oppressed, the poor,
Who when the disaster
Is declared done,
Still suffer more than anyone.

Climate change is the single greatest challenge of our time,

Of this, you're certainly aware.
It's saddening, but I cannot spare you
From knowing an inconvenient fact, because
It's getting the facts straight that gets us to act and not to wait.

So I tell you this not to scare you,
But to prepare you, to dare you
To dream a different reality,

Where despite disparities
We all care to protect this world,
This riddled blue marble, this little true marvel
To muster the verve and the nerve
To see how we can serve
Our planet. You don't need to be a politician
To make it your mission to conserve, to protect,
To preserve that one and only home
That is ours,
To use your unique power
To give next generations the planet they deserve.

We are demonstrating, creating, advocating

We heed this inconvenient truth, because we need to be anything but lenient
With the future of our youth.

And while this is a training,
in sustaining the future of our planet,
There is no rehearsal. The time is
Now
Now
Now,
Because the reversal of harm,
And protection of a future so universal
Should be anything but controversial.

So, earth, pale blue dot
We will fail you not.

Just as we chose to go to the moon
We know it's never too soon
To choose hope.
We choose to do more than cope
With climate change
We choose to end it—
We refuse to lose.
Together we do this and more
Not because it's very easy or nice
But because it is necessary,
Because with every dawn we carry
the weight of the fate of this celestial body orbiting a star.
And as heavy as that weight sounded, it doesn't hold us down,
But it keeps us grounded, steady, ready,
Because an environmental movement of this size
Is simply another form of an earthrise.

To see it, close your eyes.
Visualize that all of us leaders in this room
and outside of these walls or in the halls, all
of us changemakers are in a spacecraft,
Floating like a silver raft
in space, and we see the face of our planet anew.
We relish the view;
We witness its round green and brilliant blue,
Which inspires us to ask deeply, wholly:
What can we do?
Open your eyes.
Know that the future of
this wise planet
Lies right in sight:
Right in all of us. Trust
this earth uprising.
All of us bring light to exciting solutions never tried before
For it is our hope that implores us, at our uncompromising core,
To keep rising up for an earth more than worth fighting for.

Step 03 — Decoding

Tap & Decode: Close Analysis

Tap a quote to reveal the analytical question. Think first, then check your interpretation.

"Our planet looked like an earthrise / A blue orb hovering over the moon's gray horizon" +

Opening strategy — Ethos & Pathos

Why does Gorman choose to anchor a climate poem in a space mission from 1968?

Correct. The "Earthrise" photograph is the poem's central objective correlative: an image that embodies the emotion Gorman wants to evoke — awe, responsibility, and unity. Starting in 1968 also implies a long history of inaction: we saw the planet's fragility over 50 years ago. What have we done since?
"This riddled blue marble, this little true marvel" +

Word choice — Semantic tension

The word "riddled" (= percé de trous, rongé) disrupts the romantic image of Earth. What is the effect of this semantic shock?

Exactly. "Riddled" usually describes something perforated by bullets or disease. The violent connotation invades the romantic metaphor of the "blue marble", forcing the listener to hold beauty and destruction simultaneously. This is the emotional core of the poem: the Earth is worth fighting for because it is already suffering.
"There is no rehearsal. The time is
Now
Now
Now," +

Form as meaning — Enjambment & urgency

What is the combined visual and auditory effect of this layout?

Yes. Form enacts meaning here. On the page, the vertical stacking creates visual urgency — a countdown, a falling. In performance, it imposes silence between each "Now", making the word heavier each time it lands. The metaphor of "no rehearsal" — borrowed from theatre — is then enacted by the poem itself: there is no second take.
"For it is the obscure, the oppressed, the poor / Who … still suffer more than anyone." +

Political dimension — Environmental justice

Many climate speeches focus on "all of humanity". Why does Gorman instead name specific groups? What does this add to the poem's message?

Exactly right. This stanza is where Gorman's poem departs from mainstream environmentalism. By naming "the obscure, the oppressed, the poor", she insists that climate change is also a question of power. The wealthy can insulate themselves from disaster; the marginalised cannot. Her poem demands both ecological and social justice — they are inseparable.
"Just as we chose to go to the moon / We know it's never too soon / To choose hope." +

Historical parallel — Logos & Pathos

How does the reference to the Moon landing function as a rhetorical argument?

Brilliant. This is a logos argument hidden inside a poetic allusion. The Moon landing counters the most powerful objection to climate action: that the problem is "too big". Gorman turns the poem's opening image (Earth seen from space) into its closing argument: the same ambition that took us away from Earth must now be turned toward saving it.

Step 05 — Evaluation

Critical Thinking

Move beyond describing what Gorman does — evaluate how and how effectively she does it.

① The Volta: Where is the turn?

In poetry, the volta marks a pivotal shift in tone, register, or argument. Gorman uses this technique deliberately mid-poem. Identify the exact line where the shift occurs, and explain why she isolates it from the surrounding stanzas.

💡 Tip: Look for the one sentence written in plain prose rather than verse — a single isolated line that reads like a news headline.

② Audience & Address

Gorman uses "you", "we", and "us" at different moments. Each pronoun creates a different relationship with the listener. Trace the shift between these pronouns through the poem — what effect does each produce?

💡 Tip: When does "I tell you" become "we choose"? What changes when Gorman moves from addressing the audience to including herself among them?

③ The Limits of Optimism

Some critics argue that climate poetry risks becoming complicit in "green optimism" — offering emotional catharsis without demanding structural change. The audience feels moved, applauds, and goes home unchanged.

💡 Key vocabulary: catharsis · mobilisation · performative · systemic · individual vs. structural action

Persuasion Spectrum

How strongly does each passage appeal to Logos (reason), Pathos (emotion), or Ethos (authority/credibility)?

Pathos intensity

"A glimpse into our planet's mirror, / And as threats drew nearer, / Our own urgency became clearer"

The accumulating rhyme (mirror / nearer / clearer) creates a ratchet-effect of escalating emotion. The mirror metaphor implies self-recognition — we are not watching a disaster unfold "out there" but seeing ourselves reflected in it.

Logos intensity

"It's getting the facts straight that gets us to act and not to wait."

Gorman acknowledges the empirical dimension: facts, not feelings, drive action. This single line provides rational scaffolding for the emotional architecture around it — she is not asking for blind faith.

Ethos intensity

"pale blue dot"

A two-word allusion to Carl Sagan places Gorman in a lineage of scientific and philosophical authority. She is not an activist with an opinion — she is heir to a tradition of rational humanism that stretches from Sagan to NASA. Ethos borrowed, not just claimed.
